Case 01

Mixed Methods Survey + Interviews

Trust Is Not the Same as Reliance

Navigating information inside communities you're leaving

The Question

Teams assume that if users trust a source they rely on it, and if they distrust it they leave. I studied people living with that tension to map the real relationship between trust, reliance, and voice.

My Role

Sole researcher — entire study, both phases, end to end (doctoral capstone).

Approach

  1. Fielded a national survey of 305 women across Christian traditions.
  2. Measured trust in sources, digital + offline engagement, attitudes.
  3. Ran 20 in-depth interviews (purposive, maximum-variation sampling).
  4. Survey mapped what patterns existed; interviews explained why.
305 + 20
surveyed, then interviewed in depth

What I Found

  • A trust–reliance gap: people relied on sources they trusted less, and trusted sources they used less.
  • Compensatory engagement: those silent in their core community were the most active elsewhere.
  • Digital spaces became transition infrastructure — where people rehearsed and enacted leaving.

Recommendations

  • Measure reliance and trust separately — a single "trust" score misleads product decisions.
  • Track where suppressed behavior goes, not just where it stops.
  • Design for users in transition, not only steady states — that support earns deep loyalty.

For a marketplaceA pro who goes quiet hasn't necessarily stopped depending on you — and a pro who answers politely may already be leaving. Trust and reliance need separate instruments, or churn arrives unannounced.

ReflectionStudying a sensitive population as an outsider sharpened how I scope recruitment and build trust with vulnerable users.

Case 02

Quantitative Survey Modeling

Same Distrust, Different Roots

Why one-size-fits-all fixes fail

The Question

When users lose confidence in a system, the instinct is one universal fix. But what if different segments distrust for entirely different reasons? I tested whether one behavior had one cause — or many.

My Role

Lead researcher and first author — survey design, measurement, modeling, interpretation.

Approach

  1. Fielded a survey of 1,294 U.S. adults, quota-matched to the population.
  2. Measured each construct with validated multi-item scales; checked reliability.
  3. Modeled with SEM + bootstrapped mediation (5,000 resamples).
  4. Re-ran the model by segment to test whether mechanisms held.
1,294
participants · 5,000 bootstrap resamples

What I Found

  • On the surface distrust looked uniform — one shared driver lowered confidence everywhere.
  • But mechanisms diverged by segment: one group's distrust ran through how they evaluate information; another's did not.
  • Self-reported "skill" predicted overconfidence, not accuracy.

Recommendations

  • Segment before you intervene — the same behavior with different roots needs different solutions.
  • Don't take self-reported competence at face value.
  • Aggregate metrics can hide opposing segment effects — always disaggregate.

For a marketplaceThumbtack pros and StreetFair pros can post the same satisfaction score for entirely different reasons. Segment the mechanism before you ship the fix.

ReflectionCross-sectional, post-election data limited causal claims; next: longitudinal + behavioral (not self-report) measures.

Case 03

Qualitative Generative

Safe Enough to Speak

What makes marginalized users participate in online communities

The Question

Mainstream comment spaces are often hostile, and marginalized users leave first. I studied the inverse: what makes a community feel safe enough to participate — and what design conditions create that feeling?

My Role

Sole researcher — scoping, recruitment, discussion guide, interviews, analysis, synthesis.

Approach

  1. Screened the top LGBTQIA+ communities (Muck Rack) down to nine active forums.
  2. Recruited via a 6.9k-member group: 204 applicants → 22 users selected.
  3. Designed a 36-question discussion guide, refined with three experts.
  4. Ran ~45-min sessions; analyzed via a seven-step thematic coding process.
22
users interviewed, from 204 screened applicants

What I Found

  • Perceived similarity (18/22) was the strongest driver of participation.
  • Reciprocity (14/22) sustained active engagement over time.
  • Users who felt similarity rated the space as safe and civil (20/22).

Recommendations

  • Treat safety as a perception problem, not only moderation — make community composition and shared-identity signals visible.
  • Surface similarity signals (onboarding, profiles, matching) to convert lurkers into contributors.
  • Weigh the homogeneity tradeoff (comfort vs. lost cross-cutting exposure) as a monitored platform-health risk.

For a marketplaceThe conditions that turn lurkers into participants are designable. The same logic applies to pros deciding whether a new platform is worth showing up for.

ReflectionRandom selection converged on a single racial group — I now build quota-based screening in up front.

StreetFair × Thumbtack

The questions I'd bring to week one

Not answers — I haven't earned those yet. But this is where I'd start.

Question 01

What makes this worth an hour to a pro whose hour is billable?

Research on StreetFair's pros has to run at the pace of their workday. Before any instrument, I'd want field time — call windows between jobs, sessions that respect the invoice — because the recruiting method is itself a finding about what these operators value.

Question 02

Which parts of the StreetFair model are load-bearing?

Route density, neighbor trust, paying after the completed job — which of these does a pro's loyalty actually rest on, and which would they let go? That answer decides which tools the pod builds first, and which parts of the model the integration must not break.

Question 03

How do you measure trust that was never designed to be measured?

StreetFair worked because neighbors vouched — the platform never had to ask. As the integration starts drawing on that trust, pros are precisely the people who will say "it's fine" right up until the day they leave. Separating the polite answer from the true one is instrument design, and it's the center of my research life.
